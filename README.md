## Overview

The **Global Circulation System Restoration Plan** is an integrated environmental restoration framework that reinterprets the climate crisis not merely as a greenhouse gas emissions problem, but as a degradation of the global circulation system, including oceans, atmosphere, forests, soil, cities, water cycles, and the biosphere.

At the center of this plan are two major technological systems.

1. **Ocean Breathing System**  
   A system that uses deep-sea aeration, nanobubbles, induced upwelling, and nutrient circulation to complement ocean vertical circulation and enhance CO2 fixation by phytoplankton.

2. **Urban Environmental Improvement System**  
   A system that integrates urban soil restoration, rooftop and wall greening, organic resource circulation, water circulation infrastructure, and distributed energy to transform cities from emission sources into hubs of carbon fixation, cooling, and circulation.

This plan treats global environmental restoration not as a single technology, but as a comprehensive civilization design that includes natural laws, urban structure, ocean circulation, economic feasibility, risk management, and implementation roadmaps.

## Abstract

The modern climate crisis cannot be explained only by the increase in CO2 concentration. Weakening ocean vertical circulation, expansion of oxygen-deficient zones, increasing forest fires, soil degradation, permafrost thawing, urban heat islands, and the disruption of water cycles interact with one another and weaken the resilience of the Earth system as a whole.

This plan regards the Earth as one connected circulation system and proposes restoration technologies for both the oceanic domain, which covers roughly 70% of the Earth’s surface, and the terrestrial domain, which covers roughly 30%. In the ocean, the breathing function is supported. On land, cities and soils are transformed into circular systems. Through this combined approach, the plan aims to advance CO2 reduction and CO2 absorption simultaneously while integrating climate stabilization, ecosystem recovery, and urban environmental improvement.

This document is not merely a paper to read. It is a **blueprint for implementation** so that researchers, engineers, governments, companies, and citizens can participate in verification and improvement.

## Fundamental Philosophy: Four Principles of Human Survival

This plan is based on four principles derived from natural laws.

### 1. Providence

Nature has sustainable laws that exist beyond human convenience. Water circulates, life depends on interdependence, oceans absorb heat and carbon, and soil is regenerated by microorganisms. Human civilization cannot survive for long if it continues to violate these principles.

### 2. Harmony

Environmental restoration cannot be achieved by a single technology or a single policy. A structure is needed in which oceans, atmosphere, forests, cities, soil, economy, and society operate in harmony.

### 3. Circulation

In nature, fallen leaves, microorganisms, water, and carbon are all parts of circulation. The problem of modern civilization is that it has broken these cycles and consumed resources in a one-way flow.

### 4. Structure

Ideas alone cannot restore the global environment. Structural design is required, including ocean devices, urban infrastructure, water circulation, energy systems, manufacturing, economics, and governance.

## Problem Structure of Modern Civilization

### 1. Limits of the Linear Consumption System

Modern civilization has depended on a linear model of extraction, consumption, and disposal. While this model has produced short-term economic growth, it has also caused resource depletion, waste accumulation, CO2 emissions, and soil degradation at the same time.

### 2. Loss of Integrated Perspective through Specialization

Climate, oceans, cities, agriculture, energy, and economics are originally connected. However, modern institutions and research fields are often divided into narrow specialties, making it difficult to see the whole as one circulation structure.

### 3. Decline of Ocean Absorption Capacity

The ocean is the Earth’s largest carbon and heat absorption system. However, surface warming, stratification, and reduced mixing with deeper water make it harder for nutrients to reach the surface layer, which can weaken phytoplankton activity.

### 4. Cities as Heat and Waste Discharge Structures

Modern cities store heat through asphalt, concrete, glass, and metal. They prevent rainwater from infiltrating the ground, incinerate organic matter, and depend heavily on external energy. Without transforming cities, the restoration of global circulation will remain incomplete.

## Technical Proposal 1: Ocean Breathing System

### Basic Principle

The **Ocean Breathing System** is a technological concept that sends air into deep or mid-depth ocean layers and uses fine bubbles, nanobubbles, and induced upwelling to support weakened ocean vertical circulation.

When nutrients do not sufficiently reach the surface ocean, phytoplankton productivity declines. This weakens CO2 fixation capacity and the primary productivity of marine ecosystems. The Ocean Breathing System aims to complement nutrient movement and oxygen supply, thereby helping the ocean regain its breathing function.

### Main Components

- **Deep-Sea Aeration:** A concept for sending air to ocean depths of around 250 meters or deeper, depending on the test site and environmental conditions.
- **Nanobubble Technology:** Fine bubbles are used to improve oxygen supply and residence time in seawater.
- **Spiral Outlet:** A vortex-generating outlet improves mixing efficiency.
- **Octahedral Platform:** A marine structure that integrates solar, wind, and tidal power generation.
- **Autonomous Operation:** Sensors and AI control adjust operation according to environmental data.

### Expected Effects

- Support for ocean vertical circulation
- Nutrient supply to the surface layer
- Activation of phytoplankton
- Improvement of CO2 fixation capacity
- Mitigation of oxygen-deficient zones
- Recovery of marine ecosystems
- Supplementary contribution to climate stabilization

### Implementation Caution

Intervention in the ocean must begin with small-scale verification. Risks such as red tides, harmful algal blooms, local ecosystem changes, effects on fisheries, and consistency with international law must be carefully examined before broader deployment.

## Technical Proposal 2: Urban Environmental Improvement System

### Basic Principle

The **Urban Environmental Improvement System** transforms cities from centers of emissions and waste heat into hubs of carbon fixation, water circulation, greening, food production, and distributed energy.

Cities are a major cause of global environmental stress, but they also offer one of the greatest opportunities for improvement. By changing urban structures, human activity itself can transition toward circularity.

### Main Components

#### 1. Humus and Composting Facilities

Food residues, fallen leaves, pruned branches, and kitchen waste should not be incinerated. They should be reused as humus, compost, or soil improvement materials. This reduces CO2 emissions and waste heat from incineration while securing soil materials for urban greening and agricultural restoration.

#### 2. Urban Soil Restoration

Soil and plants should be restored to rooftops, walls, vacant lots, roadside spaces, parks, schools, and public facilities. This is not merely decorative greening. It aims to provide water retention, evapotranspiration cooling, carbon fixation, and biodiversity recovery.

#### 3. Water Circulation Infrastructure

Rainwater harvesting, reclaimed water use, seawater desalination, purification and reuse, urban waterways, and underground water storage should be combined. Urban water should not simply be discharged. It should be circulated for cooling, irrigation, purification, and reuse.

#### 4. Distributed Energy

Vertical-axis wind turbines, solar power, small hydropower, and waste heat utilization should be distributed throughout the city. Buildings and waterways themselves can become sites of energy recovery.

#### 5. Eco-District Development

Parts of cities can be redesigned as eco-districts that integrate greening, water circulation, renewable energy, regional resource circulation, and urban agriculture. Expanding these districts step by step can improve the circularity of the entire city.

## Risk Management

This plan is a technology for complementing natural circulation. However, any environmental intervention requires careful verification.

### Ocean-Related Risks

- Potential red tides or harmful algal blooms
- Local changes in nutrient balance
- Excess or insufficient oxygen supply
- Effects on fisheries and shipping
- Effects on currents and water temperature structure
- Long-term ecosystem changes

### Urban-Related Risks

- Sanitation management in composting facilities
- Odor and pest control
- Building load and structural safety
- Water quality management
- Maintenance costs
- Consensus-building with residents

### Integrated Risk Management

Implementation should always include observation, stop conditions, third-party verification, local agreement, and data disclosure. Sharing failures and side effects, as well as successes, improves the reliability of the entire technology system.
